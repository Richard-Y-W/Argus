# 2026-07-11 — Two clocks (EXP-003)

Second cycle of the day. EXP-002 left the calendar-time confound as the binding threat: if all characteristic returns simply attenuated in the 2000s, "post-publication" is just "recent" wearing a lab coat. Chordia–Subrahmanyam–Tong (2014) is the strongest published version of that view — anomaly profits halved after 2001 decimalization, attributed to liquidity and arbitrage capital.

The C&Z panel has something CST's 12 anomalies do not: publication years staggered across five decades, so the calendar and each predictor's own clock come apart. HYP-003 registered the era bins, event-time bins, and six predictions before any era-controlled regression ran; the uncontrolled benchmarks were already public knowledge inside the lab, and the design says so.

Result: the predictor's own clock wins, and not narrowly. The publication coefficient barely moves when era dummies enter (−0.369 → −0.325, t = −2.97); a linear trend does nothing; 5-year bins do nothing. The registered 2001–2010 era effect — the CST prediction — came back +0.02 with a t of 0.13. Three of six registered predictions failed (no era effect; decay flat rather than deepening in event time; placebo drift unresolved), and the failures are where today's understanding actually advanced:

- **P2's failure reframes a published result.** CST couldn't separate the clocks because every anomaly in their set was published before their break. When the clocks are collinear in a sample, the sample cannot tell you which one rings. Our panel can, and it says event time.
- **P4's failure sharpens the mechanism picture:** the drop arrives within five years of publication and stays — arbitrage acts fast; there is no slow capital ramp visible at this resolution.
- **P6's failure is a lesson in power:** the placebo drift kept its point estimate (−0.09) but lost its significance under era controls. We registered a magnitude threshold and missed it. The right response is not to argue with the threshold but to design the discriminating test — correlation-sorted placebo decay, now the EXP-004 candidate and the researcher's open prediction question.

One unregistered surprise flagged without interpretation: the only significant era effect is *positive* — predictors earned +0.22 %/mo more in 1993–2000 conditional on windows.

Where the thread stands after three experiments: replication (EXP-001), control group (EXP-002), clock decomposition (EXP-003) — the McLean–Pontiff causal story has survived everything this lab can throw at it with free data. The open edges are spillover (EXP-004), the SSRN working-paper profile, and the parked US-only puzzle.

Housekeeping honesty: three of six registered predictions failing in one cycle is a feature, not an embarrassment — but it is also a calibration signal. Argus's registered ranges have now missed on P4 (EXP-002) and P2/P4/P6 (EXP-003), all in the direction of over-predicting mechanism visibility.
