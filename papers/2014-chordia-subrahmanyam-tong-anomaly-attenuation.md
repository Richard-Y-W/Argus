# Chordia, Subrahmanyam & Tong (2014) — Have capital market anomalies attenuated in the recent era of high liquidity and trading activity?

*Journal of Accounting and Economics 58(1): 41–58. Note written 2026-07-11 by Argus from the abstract, SSRN page, and secondary summaries — not a full read; flagged accordingly. Read for: EXP-003 motivation (calendar-time attenuation).*

## Hypothesis
Anomaly profits shrink as arbitrage gets cheaper and more abundant — a *calendar-time* story: what matters is the trading-technology and capital era, not each paper's publication date.

## Framework / design
Twelve prominent US anomalies (size, value, momentum, beta, accruals, turnover, among others). Attenuation is dated to *common* calendar events and state variables: decimalization (tick size drop, January 2001) as a quasi-exogenous liquidity shock, plus hedge-fund AUM, aggregate short interest, and aggregate share turnover as arbitrage-activity proxies.

## Headline findings (as reported; not independently verified)
- Returns to a composite 12-anomaly strategy roughly **halve after decimalization**.
- Attenuation is stronger where/when hedge-fund AUM, short interest, and turnover are higher.
- Interpretation: liquidity and arbitrage capital, not academic publication per se, drive the decline.

## Assumptions and limitations
- A single structural break (2001) plus trending aggregates makes almost *any* decline "line up" — the identification is coarser than M&P's staggered publication dates.
- Anomaly set is small (12) and old; all were published well before 2001, so publication-timing and era effects are nearly collinear *in their sample*. The C&Z panel, with publications staggered 1970s–2010s, is better suited to separate the two.
- Same US-only scope as the rest of the thread.

## Criticisms / open questions
- CST's story and M&P's are not rivals so much as competing *attributions of timing*: M&P's own robustness (time controls) says publication matters beyond trend, and Brogaard–Nguyen–Putniņš–Zhang (2023 WP, "What drives anomaly decay?") finds **both** channels contribute — publication effects strongest for non-accounting anomalies, liquidity effects for accounting-based ones.
- For our thread: EXP-002's placebo drift (−0.11 %/mo post-"publication") is exactly what a mild CST-style era effect would leave in a group with no predictive claim.
