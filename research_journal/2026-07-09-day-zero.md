## Open decisions for the researcher (before the first research cycle)

1. **Baseline assessment.** Where does the researcher currently stand on the mathematics ladder (probability → time series → econometrics → stochastic calculus)? The first learning plan depends on this.
2. **First cycle: replication vs. exploration.** The strong prior from research-apprenticeship practice is to start with a *replication* of a landmark result (e.g., Jegadeesh & Titman 1993 momentum, Fama & French 1993 factors, or the low-volatility anomaly). Replication forces contact with real data problems — survivorship bias, delisting returns, look-ahead — without also requiring a novel idea. But the choice should engage the researcher's own interest: intrinsic curiosity compounds.
3. **Data access.** What data sources are available (CRSP/Compustat via school? Free sources like Ken French's data library, FRED, Yahoo)? Ken French's library alone is sufficient for several serious replications and is fully reproducible — a good constraint for cycle one.

## A question to sit with (mentor mode, from day one)

> Before reading anything: why do *you* think a published anomaly might stop working after publication? Write down your guess in `ideas/` before we read McLean & Pontiff (2016). The gap between your prior and the literature is the first measurement of the apprenticeship.
