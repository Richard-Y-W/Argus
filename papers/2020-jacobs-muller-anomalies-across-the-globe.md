# Jacobs & Müller (2020) — Anomalies Across the Globe: Once Public, No Longer Existent?

*Journal of Financial Economics 135(1), 213–230. SSRN 2816490. Note by Argus 2026-07-10.*

## Main question

Does the McLean–Pontiff post-publication decay replicate outside the United States?

## Design

241 cross-sectional anomalies constructed in 39 stock markets (>2 million anomaly country-months); same three-window logic as M&P applied per country.

## Key results

- **United States**: strong confirmation of M&P — roughly 38% post-sample and ~62% post-publication decline (equal-weighted; similar value-weighted).
- **Everywhere else**: **no reliable post-publication decline.** The US is the only market where publication predictably kills returns.
- Interpretation offered: barriers to arbitrage segment markets; the persistence of anomalies abroad (where they were never "discovered" locally at scale) suggests anomalies are **mispricing rather than data mining** — data-mined noise would fail out-of-sample everywhere, not persist abroad.

## Why this is the interesting puzzle

The international evidence is an *independent out-of-sample test* of the data-mining explanation: if published US anomalies were noise, they should not work in Japan or Germany at all. They mostly do — and keep working after publication there. So decay looks like an **arbitrage-capital phenomenon localized to the most institutionally efficient market**, not a statistical artifact. This sharpens, rather than settles, the mechanism question.

## Limitations / open questions

- International data quality and shorter samples reduce power; "no reliable decline" is not "reliably no decline."
- Why don't global arbitrageurs export the correction? Capital immobility, mandate constraints, shorting frictions abroad — untested decomposition.
- Publication venue is US-centric; local-language dissemination is unmeasured.
