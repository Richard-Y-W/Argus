# 2026-07-12 — The first rejection, and why it was the best cycle yet

Richard asked for another autonomous cycle "with something else." EXP-004 stays behind his worksheet gate, so the cycle went to the obvious open flank: *which* predictors decay. M&P ran this in 2016; we had 188 predictors, an original-paper t-stat column M&P had to hand-collect, a citations column they couldn't have, and three experiments' worth of validated machinery. Registration to results took one session.

The registered analysis behaved exactly like a mechanism story wants to: strong-evidence predictors decay more (t = −5.1), volatile ones decay more (t = −3.2), everything robust to eras, cohorts, winsorizing. A day-one version of this lab would have shipped that as a success. The tercile table saved us: the *proportional* decline was 50% / 49% / 55% across t-stat terciles — suspiciously flat for a story about capital hunting the strongest signals. One labeled post-hoc regressor later (pp × in-sample mean, t = −5.2), both registered interactions were at zero and HYP-005 was dead. Decay is a haircut, not a targeting decision: publication costs a predictor about half of whatever it had.

Three things worth the ink:

1. **A registered prediction passed and was still wrong.** P1 met every registered criterion; the specification was simply aimed at levels when the phenomenon lives in ratios. Registration disciplines *claims*; it does not aim the rifle. The failed_experiments directory finally has its first citizen, and it earned the spot by being more informative than any of the three successes.
2. **The failure has the same fingerprint as Argus's previous prediction misses** — over-predicting mechanism visibility. That's now 6 of 16 registered predictions across four experiments, all in one direction. The calibration note in the scorecard graduates from observation to standing rule: Argus mechanism predictions are optimistic until a scale/composition diagnostic has run.
3. **The rejection sharpens EXP-004 rather than crowding it.** If decay is proportional to in-sample edge, and placebos have no edge, then shrinkage predicts ~zero placebo drift — so whatever drift Richard's correlation sort finds (if any) is much more cleanly spillover than it was yesterday. His worksheet question got easier to answer and harder to dodge. The digest hands him that scaffold and two numbers to commit to.

Loose ends filed: sample-length sort (splits proportional arbitrage from luck-reversion — luck shrinks with √n, edge doesn't); registered VW/EW test of the near-total correction in value-weighted ports (94% vs 55%, descriptive, n = 28); re-read M&P Tables 5–6 for the same scale confound.

Researcher absent this cycle; attribution AI-led. The worksheet gate held: nothing in EXP-005 touches placebo correlations.
